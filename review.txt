### 1. First Impression & Navigation UX

  • The 3-Second Test: You pass the 3-second test, but just barely. Your headline ("Building resilient systems and scalable architecture") and badge ("System Admin &
  Software Developer") are strong. However, you immediately shoot yourself in the foot by leading your description with "I am a 21-year-old software engineering
  student..." Stop doing that. I am hiring engineers, not students. Lead with your value: "Backend Engineer specializing in robust backend development and systems
  architecture..."
  • Friction & Flow: A single-page layout is excellent. I don't want to click through 5 pages to figure out what you do. However, you are missing the most critical
  call-to-action a recruiter looks for: A clear, obvious "Download Resume/CV" button. You have a "View Architecture & Projects" button and some social links, but if I
  want to download your PDF to put into my ATS (Applicant Tracking System), I shouldn't have to hunt for it.

  ### 2. The "Noise" (What We Don't Care About)

  You've avoided the cardinal sin of "Skill Progress Bars" (thank god), but you still have fluff weighing you down:

  • High School & CPGE in Education: Delete "Baccalauréat" and "Classe Préparatoire" immediately. Nobody cares what high school you went to when you are deploying
  enterprise Spring Boot apps and writing bare-metal Rust. It screams "junior." Keep the BUT and the Engineering degree.
  • Basic API Fetching Projects: You have a custom Bitcoin node in Rust and declarative NixOS environments... and then you have "GameList" (a Next.js app fetching
  data from the RAWG API). The latter looks like a weekend bootcamp tutorial. It dilutes the absolute powerhouse systems-level work you've done. Cut the generic web
  apps and lean heavily into your complex, infrastructure-heavy projects.

  ### 3. The "Missing Pieces" (What Is Not Told)

  • Buried Experience (The biggest red flag): You casually mention in a wall of text that you "architected an enterprise automated testing framework" and are "joining
  the core team to rewrite... internal framework modules." Where is your "Experience" section? Recruiters skim; we do not read paragraphs. You are hiding actual, paid,
  enterprise impact inside an "About Me" block. Extract this into a dedicated timeline that shows the company name, your title, and dates.
  • Zero Impact Metrics: You built an enterprise testing framework. Great. Did it run 10,000 tests a night? Did it reduce QA manual testing hours by 40%? Did it
  increase deployment confidence? I need numbers, scale, and business impact. Without metrics, it's just a task list.
  • The "Why" in Projects: Your project descriptions tell me what you built ("A minimalist Bitcoin node"), but not why or how it proves you're a good engineer. Add
  one sentence to each project detailing the hardest technical hurdle you overcame (e.g., "Implemented custom TCP handshakes for peer discovery" or "Handled
  concurrent UTXO state-locking").

  ### 4. Bottom Line & Quick Wins

  • Hireability Score: Medium-High (You have the raw technical chops of a High-tier candidate, but your portfolio is packaged like a standard junior, dragging your
  score down).

  1. Extract your Apprenticeship into an "Experience" section. Bullet points, action verbs, and hard metrics (percentages, numbers, scale). Stop hiding your real-
  world experience in a paragraph.
  2. Add a "Download Resume" CTA directly in the Hero section next to the "View Projects" button. Make it impossible for me to leave without your PDF.
  3. Trim the Fat. Remove high school from your timeline, drop "GameList" and "Bagarre.io" from your featured work, and own your identity as a hardcore
